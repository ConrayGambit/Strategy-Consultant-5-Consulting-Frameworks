# "So What?" Test — EdTech

A standalone run of the `so-what-test` skill on an EdTech learning-outcome problem.

---

## Input (what the user typed)

> We run an online coding bootcamp. Our 12-week course completion rate has fallen from 71% to 53% over four cohorts. I've got data showing the drop-off is happening between week 3 and week 5. I need to translate this into something I can present to the founders Friday — they want a clear recommendation, not a status update.

---

## Output (what the skill produced)

**Process:** Analyzed completion rates across the last four cohorts (n = 612 total students); segmented drop-off by week; cross-referenced drop-off patterns with curriculum changes, instructor cohorts, and cohort demographics; ran exit-survey analysis on the 287 students who dropped between weeks 3 and 5.

**Result:** 64% of all dropouts occur between week 3 and week 5, when the curriculum transitions from frontend HTML/CSS/JS to backend Node + databases. Drop-off in this window has nearly doubled (from 14% to 26%) since the curriculum was restructured nine months ago to compress two backend modules into one. Exit-survey responses cluster around two themes: "the pace was overwhelming once we hit databases" (cited by 51% of leavers) and "I couldn't get help fast enough" (cited by 38%). Other variables — instructor cohort, time of year, student demographics, prior-experience mix — show no statistically significant correlation with drop-off rates.

**Insight:** The completion drop is not a marketing problem, a recruiting problem, or a generalized "students aren't motivated" problem. It is a **specific curriculum-pacing failure in weeks 3–5** caused by the backend module compression done nine months ago. Compounded by support-response time for stuck students. **Recommendation for Friday:** un-compress the backend curriculum. Restore weeks 3–5 to the prior structure (one module per week instead of compressed) and add a synchronous "office hours" block during the database week. Owner: Curriculum Lead and Head of Instruction, jointly. Target: next cohort starts in 4 weeks; pilot with that cohort and measure week-3-to-5 retention. Goal: bring the week-3-to-5 retention rate back above 80% (current: 60%), restoring overall completion to ≥65% within two cohorts. **Cost:** rebuilding curriculum content from version-controlled prior version + 1 instructor for office hours = ~$15K and 3 weeks of prep. **Decision needed Friday:** authorize the curriculum rebuild or accept the current trajectory.

---

## What to notice

- **Process is one paragraph, but specific.** It names the data, the cohorts, the segments, and what was cross-referenced. A founder reading this knows the analysis was rigorous without needing the methodology slides.
- **Result has numbers and ruled-out alternatives.** "64% of dropouts in weeks 3–5," "doubled from 14% to 26%," and the explicit statement that other variables showed no correlation. The Result rules out competing explanations before the Insight is offered.
- **Insight passes the assignability test.** Named owners (Curriculum Lead + Head of Instruction), specific action (un-compress backend, add office hours), explicit target (>80% week-3-to-5 retention), timeline (4 weeks to pilot), and cost ($15K + 3 weeks of prep). A founder could literally say "approved" and the team would know exactly what to do.
- **Decision-needed sentence at the end.** "Authorize the curriculum rebuild or accept the current trajectory." That's how strategy hands off to a decision meeting — by surfacing the binary the principals need to resolve.
- **No hedging.** Not "we should consider..." or "it might help to..." but "un-compress the backend curriculum" with a target and a cost. The founders can disagree, but they can't say the recommendation was unclear.
